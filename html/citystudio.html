Context

CityStudio is an organization that matches university courses to city staff members to create experimental projects. These projects aim to help students gain valuable real-world experience, while inspiring innovation within the City of Vancouver. Our goal? To boost student engagement for CityStudio’s independent and engagement models.

Outcome

We reframed CityStudio’s engagement challenge from a student-facing problem to a faculty-facing outreach problem, leading to a low-cost, low-risk physical outreach strategy designed to capture professor attention and enable easier course adoption.

Initial Direction & Assumptions

Based on early research and stakeholder conversations, we initially framed the problem as a student engagement issue.

Our goals were to:

1. Improve CityStudio’s outreach

2. Increase student interest in projects

3. Create structured project expectations

Using personas, we explored three early strategies:

Interactive Map

an exploratory archive of CityStudio projects for instructors

Linear Guide

a simplified, guided introduction to CityStudio and its offerings

CityQuest

a digital scavenger hunt to onboard students before collaboration

Key Learnings & Pivot

Evaluating these early directions surfaced three major insights:

1. Budget & Risk Constraints

2. Bias in Problem Framing

3. Digital Was Not the Only Path

These insights prompted a reframing of the problem—from How do we engage students? to:

“How might we increase professor awareness of CityStudio and make collaboration easy to integrate into existing courses?”

The Solution: CityStudio Integration Program

Introducing the CityStudio Integration Program

Interactive Physical Prototype

Content Within Physical Mailer

Design Rationale: Why Physical Mail

Physical outreach addressed CityStudio’s constraints while aligning with faculty behavior.

  • No competition compared to email
  • High likelihood of being noticed and retained
  • Allows professors to engage on their own terms
  • Does not force collaboration
  • Low cost and easy to update over time
  • Breaks the monotony of digital communication
However, this also introduced a constraint: it could not feel like junk mail. The content had to be purposeful, concise, and respectful of professors’ time.

Interactive Microsite Prototype

Design Rationale: Why a Microsite

A focused microsite allowed us to condense only the most relevant information, provide interactive elements such as a 13-week course timeline, and support progressive disclosure. Paired with the physical mailer, the microsite sustained engagement without requiring a full website redesign, while remaining easy to update and maintain.

Reflection

This project challenged us to confront our assumptions and redesign the problem before designing solutions.

By shifting focus from students to professors, we uncovered a more sustainable and impactful path forward for CityStudio’s engagement strategy.

However, this also introduced a constraint: it could not feel like junk mail. The content had to be purposeful, concise, and respectful of professors’ time.

Key takeaways:

1. Engagement problems often stem from the wrong target audience

2. Low-tech solutions can outperform digital ones when aligned with real behavior

3. Designing for attention is as important as designing for usability

Given more time, further refinements and tailoring content of the 13-week implementation strategy to specific courses would build a stronger sense of confidence. A further refined strategy would provide a real-world proof of concept.
